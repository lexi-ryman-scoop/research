## What's Actually Driving People Away
**Background:** White (no pattern)

### It's Not That Tableau is Bad. It's That It's Built for a Different User.

**The Learning Curve That Never Ends**
Your marketing manager doesn't have three months to master calculated fields. Your sales ops lead can't spend their weekends learning about data blending. They have pipelines to manage, campaigns to run, and bosses asking questions that needed answers last week.

Tableau assumes you'll invest the time. Most business users can't.

**The Hidden Costs**
The license fee is just the beginning. Factor in:
- Tableau Prep (because your data is never clean enough)
- Server or Cloud hosting
- Training programs
- The ongoing IT maintenance
- The analyst time spent building reports for people who can't build their own

One finance leader told us their "self-service" Tableau deployment ended up requiring a dedicated analyst just to manage dashboard requests.

**The Support Cliff**
Post-Salesforce acquisition, the support experience has changed. Reddit and G2 are full of stories about being routed through offshore support teams with scripted responses. For enterprise-level issues, you're often left digging through community forums.

## Who Should Actually Consider Leaving Tableau
**Background:** Light Gray + Sparkle pattern

### This Isn't for Everyone

**Stay with Tableau if:**
- You have dedicated data analysts who live in the tool daily
- Your team has already invested heavily in training and adoption is working
- You need extremely custom, pixel-perfect visualizations for board presentations
- You're already deep in the Salesforce ecosystem and the integration is worth it

**Look for alternatives if:**
- Your business users never got past the basics
- You're paying for licenses that aren't being used
- Every "quick question" still goes through the data team
- You need answers in hours, not days
- Your data lives across spreadsheets, CRMs, and cloud apps that don't play nice together

### Power BI
**Best for:** Organizations already committed to Microsoft

If your company runs on Microsoft 365 and your team lives in Excel, Power BI can make sense. The $10/user/month price point is significantly lower than Tableau, and the Excel integration is genuine.

**The reality check:**
DAX (Power BI's formula language) has its own learning curve. Users complain about basic things like sorting months chronologically requiring workarounds. And if you're not on Windows, the experience degrades quickly.

One G2 reviewer put it bluntly: "August, December, July… total chaos" when trying to sort data. These small frustrations add up.

**Best for teams who:**
- Are already deep in Microsoft ecosystem
- Have Excel power users ready to level up
- Prioritize cost over ease of use

---

### Looker (Google)
**Best for:** Companies with data engineering resources

Looker's strength is its modeling layer (LookML). Once your data team builds the semantic model, business users get a consistent, governed view of metrics.

**The catch:**
You need data engineers to build and maintain LookML. Implementation takes months. And the Google Cloud dependency might not fit your stack.

Looker solves a different problem than Tableau—it's about data governance and consistency, not self-service exploration. If your data team is bought in and you have the resources, it's powerful. If you're looking for faster time-to-insight, it's not the answer.

---

### ThoughtSpot
**Best for:** Enterprises with budget for search-based analytics

ThoughtSpot pioneered the "Google for your data" concept. Type a question, get a chart. The interface is genuinely easier than Tableau for simple queries.

**The reality check:**
That $50/user/month advertised price? Vendr data shows the average contract is closer to $140,000/year. And the "self-service" requires your data team to build and maintain semantic models first.

As one user put it: "I seem to get stuck at 70% of what I want to do." Another noted: "Natural language search is impossible without the data team doing a huge amount of up-front data modelling work."

ThoughtSpot is best when you have both the budget and the data engineering resources to make it sing.

---

### Metabase
**Best for:** Startups with technical founders

Open-source, free to self-host, and genuinely simpler than Tableau for basic dashboards. If you have someone technical who can set it up and maintain it, Metabase gets you 80% of the way there at a fraction of the cost.

**The limitations:**
No AI features. Limited visualization options. Self-hosting means you're responsible for uptime and security. And as your needs grow, you'll likely outgrow it.

Best for early-stage teams who need something functional while they figure out their real requirements.

## Before You Switch
**Background:** White (no pattern)

### Three Questions to Ask First

**1. Is this a tool problem or a data problem?**
Sometimes the issue isn't Tableau—it's that your data is scattered across 12 different systems with no single source of truth. A new tool won't fix that. (Though some tools, like Scoop, are built to work with messy, distributed data.)

**2. What does "success" actually look like?**
Be specific. "Better adoption" is vague. "Marketing managers can answer their own campaign performance questions without filing a ticket" is measurable. Define the outcome before shopping for tools.

**3. Who's actually going to use this?**
The features that matter for a data analyst are completely different from what matters for a sales manager. Most tools are optimized for one or the other. Make sure you're buying for your actual users, not an idealized version of them.
